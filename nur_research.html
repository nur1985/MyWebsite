MINERvA Experiment

A neutrino scattering experiment which seeks to measure low energy neutrino interactions both in support of neutrino oscillation experiments and to study the strong dynamics of the nucleon and nucleus that affect these interactions.

Neutrino-electron Scattering
Neutrino-electron elastic scattering is used as a reference process to constrain the neutrino flux at the Main Injector (NuMI) beam observed by the MINERvA experiment. Prediction of the neutrino flux at accelerator experiments from other methods has a large uncertainty, and this uncertainty degrades measurements of neutrino oscillations and neutrino cross-sections. Neutrino-electron elastic scattering is a rare process, but its cross-section is precisely known. Using our data on neutrino-electron scattering we expect to reduce MINERvA’s dominant systematic uncertainty for all the cross section analysis and any current or future experiments using NuMI beam.

DAQ and Detector Operations
MINERvA employs a fine grained scintillator detector capable of complete kinematic characterization of neutrino interactions. The DAQ, slowcontrols (SC), and near-online monitoring (nearline) systems are built around MINERnA-specific readout electronics. MINERnA utilizes plastic scintillator as its fundamental detector technology. Light is delivered via wavelength shifting fiber to multi-anode photo multiplier tubes(PMTs). Each PMT is readout by a Front End Board (FEB) that digitize and store charge using pipeline ADCs. All FEB operations are controlled by a Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) chip. Despite using more than 32,000 active channels in MINERnA, the quantity of data per neutrino spill is not large due to low interaction cross-sections. My goal as the operations co-ordinator is to ensure continuous data taking and troubleshooting any DAQ related issues.

Transverse N-to-Δ

The beam normal single spin asymmetry (Bn) is generated in the scattering of transversely polarized electrons from unpolarized nuclei. The asymmetry arises from the interference of the imaginary part of the two-photon exchange with the one-photon exchange amplitude. The Qweak experiment has made the first measurement of Bn in the production of the Δ(1232) resonance, using the Qweak apparatus in Hall-C at the Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility. The final transverse asymmetry, corrected for backgrounds and beam polarization, is Bn = 43 ± 16 ppm at beam energy 1.16 GeV at an average scattering angle of about 8.3 degrees, and invariant mass of 1.2 GeV. The measured preliminary Bn agrees with a preliminary theoretical calculation. Bn for the Δ is the only known observable that is sensitive to the Δ elastic form-factors (γ*ΔΔ) in addition to the generally studied transition form-factors (γ*NΔ), but extracting this information will require significant theoretical input.

Weak Charge Measurement of the Proton

The Q-weak experiment at the Hall-C of Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility has made the first direct measurement of the weak charge of the proton, QpW, through the precision measurement of the parity-violating asymmetry in elastic e-p scattering at low momentum transfer, Q2=0.026(GeV/c)2, with incident electron beam energy of 1.16 GeV. The Q-weak experiment, along with earlier results of the parity violating elastic scattering experiments, is expected to determine QpW with a 4.2% combined statistical and systematic error. QpW is suppressed in the Standard Model and our data will be used to determine the weak-mixing angle, sin2θW, with a relative uncertainty of 0.3% that will provide a competitive measurement of the running of sin2θW to low Q2.

Beam Modulation Syatem

The objective of the Q-weak experiment is to measure the weak charge of the proton via the parity violating asymmetry (~200 ppb) in elastic e+p scattering. The e+p scattering rate largely depends on five beam parameters: horizontal position (X), angle (X´), vertical position (Y), angle (Y´), and beam energy (E). Changes in these beam parameters when the beam polarization is reversed will create false asymmetries. Although we attempt to keep changes in beam parameters during reversal as small as possible, we must correct for such false asymmetries. To do this, we modulate X, X´, Y, Y´ using four air-core dipoles in the Hall C beamline and measure the sensitivities of the detectors to beam parameter differences. We also modulate beam energy using a superconducting RF cavity. Two air-core dipoles separated by ~10m are pulsed at a time to produce position and angle changes at the target, for virtually any tune of the beamline. Useful products include a near real-time picture of the beamline optics, a long term survey of optics stability, and linearly regressed experimental asymmetries complementary to those derived from natural beam motion.

Nuclear Transparency of Kaons

The propagation of hadrons in the nuclear medium is an essential element for the understanding of the nuclear many-body system and quasifree electron scattering from nuclei provides an excellent tool for a microscopic examination of such effects in the nuclear medium. The proton propagation in nuclei has been studied extensively over the past two decades and, pion propagation in nuclei was also studied by using exclusive electroproduction of pions from nuclei at Hall-C of Jfferson Lab to find the first evidence of the novel phenomena known as color transparency. However, the pion data also include some kaons, which were isolated and analyzed in our study. The kaon transparencies were extracted by comparing the electro-production of kaons from various nuclear targets to electro-production from deuterium. The cross section for dfferent targets were compared with standard Hall-C monte carlo simulation (SIMC) in order to extract nuclear transparency. Effective kaon-nucleon cross-sections were extracted from the transparencies of kaons from this dataset and from existing electro-production world data of pions, and protons. The energy and nucleon number dependence of the transparency was compared to the results obtained from kaon-nucleus scattering data.

Nuclear Data Structure and the Decay Scheme

The various properties of nuclides always attracted me and I got an opportunity to acquire extensive experience on nuclear data structure and the decay scheme of the mass-chain A = 225 during my master degree in physics at IIT Roorkee. In this project we wrote programs on the basis of new data available on that mass-chain to date, examined their different decay schemes, and analyzed them to update the database of the National Nuclear Data Center (NNDC) at Brookhaven National Laboratory (BNL). Experimental data and theoretical calculations for eight elements of the mass chain were evaluated using NNDC softwares. In the evaluation process, detailed evaluated level properties and related information were presented, including adopted values of level and γ-ray energies, decay scheme, and other spectroscopic properties. This work has helped us to build a set of critically evaluated properties of nuclides based on the best known experimental and theoretical informations to date.
